CORTRE initial item list

Modified

March 25, 2026

ImportantFirst version — Delphi consensus

This page presents version 1 of the initial candidate item list for the CORTRE reporting guideline. It is intended as the starting point for a Delphi exercise: we seek consensus among stakeholders on which items to retain, merge, split, or drop, and how they should be worded. This is not a final guideline; content and numbering may change.

Terminology

Reproducibility effort. With reproducibility effort we refer to a project that investigates an aspect of reproducibility. In the literature, different aspects of reproducibility are referred to with different terms, such as replicability, robustness, or generalisability. This reporting guideline can be applied to efforts investigating any one of those aspects of reproducibility. A reproducibility effort typically consists of multiple individual reproducibility studies. In the following, we use the term reproduction to refer to any kind of such individual reproducibility study. However, this does not imply that this terminology should be adopted for reporting your reproducibility effort.

Original study. We use the term original study broadly to refer to that which is being reproduced, e.g. original publications, experiments, studies, results, claims, hypotheses, or outcomes. If the difference between these latter terms is relevant for your reproducibility effort, indicate this accordingly in each reporting item.

Purpose and type of the effort

  1. What was the purpose of your reproducibility effort?

  2. What is the general design / architecture of your reproducibility effort? If applicable, include a flowchart.

  3. Did you reanalyze existing data from original studies or did your reproductions collect their own data?

  4. Did you aim to follow the experimental and/or analysis methods of the original studies as closely as possible, or were variations deliberately incorporated?

  5. Did you use a conceptual or terminological framework for terms such as “reproduction” or “replication”? If so, indicate which framework you use, and how you situate your reproducibility effort within this framework.

  6. In what way did you label your study as a reproducibility effort in the title of your publication?

Selection of original studies

  1. How did you select which original study or studies to reproduce? This includes how you selected:

    1. one or multiple original publications,

    2. one or multiple experiments, studies, hypotheses, outcomes, or results within the original publication(s) (if applicable)

  2. How many original studies did you plan or attempt to reproduce, and how was that number determined?

  3. How many of the planned reproductions were you actually able to conduct?

  4. Did you analyze any descriptive statistics (e.g. research field, year of publication, journals, author demographics) of the set of original studies, and if so which ones?

Reproductions

  1. How many reproductions did you conduct for each original study, and how did you determine that number?

  2. If there are multiple teams (groups, labs) carrying out the reproductions, with what recruitment process and subject to what selection criteria were they chosen?

  3. What instructions were the reproduction teams given?

  4. If there were multiple groups reproducing the same original study, to what extent were their reproductions and protocols harmonized vs. independent of each other?

  5. Did you analyze any descriptive statistics (e.g. time of execution, demographics of reproduction teams) of the set of reproductions or of the reproduction teams, and if so which ones?

  6. Did you and/or the reproduction teams attempt to predict the reproduction outcome (overall or for individual reproductions)?

  7. What is the level of prior knowledge about the original studies, both of the reproduction teams and the authors of the overarching reproducibility effort?

Access to original studies

  1. Did your reproductions involve the original authors? If so, how?

  2. What published materials from the original studies did you use (e.g. publication, protocol, data, code)?

  3. Did you evaluate the reporting quality (e.g. completeness, data and code availability, documentation) of the published materials, and how?

  4. Did you evaluate potential biases in the original studies, and how?

  5. Did you have access to any unpublished materials from the original studies (e.g. lab notebooks, internal communications, supplier information, or information requested directly from the original authors)?

  6. Did you evaluate the quality (e.g. comprehensibility, documentation) of the unpublished materials, and if so, how?

  7. Was any unpublished material or information requested from the authors but not obtained, and if so, what exactly?

Preregistration strategy and deviations

  1. Did you preregister the protocols for the individual reproductions, and if so, when and what aspects of them?

  2. Did you preregister the protocol for the reproducibility effort (i.e. the overarching analysis of multiple individual reproductions, if applicable), and if so, when and what aspects of it?

  3. If you had preregistered the protocols of individual reproductions or the overarching analysis, were revisions allowed after the reproductions had started, and if so, until what point in their progression?

  4. If you had preregistered the protocols of individual reproductions, how did you deal with necessary deviations from the preregistrations, or revisions thereof, if any?

  5. Were researcher predictions about reproduction results collected and/or registered before reproductions were conducted (see item 16)? If so, how?

Sample size

  1. If you collected new data (see item 3), how was the sample size determined for the individual reproductions?

Adaptations from original studies

  1. If you aimed to follow the experimental and/or analysis methods of the original studies closely (see item 4), were adaptations necessary, and if so, how frequently and to what extent?

  2. If you deliberately incorporated variations from the original studies (see item 4), which aspects were varied and to what degree?

  3. Did your reproductions include additional bias control measures that were not done in the original studies?

Quality control

  1. Were reproduction teams blinded to the original studies and their results, and if so, how?

  2. What bias control measures, if any, were used in the reproductions?

  3. Were the reproductions reviewed, by whom, and at what stage? (e.g. the protocol, execution, or results)

  4. Is there a specific reporting guideline for the type(s) of original study you reproduce (e.g. CONSORT, STROBE), and was it used to report on the results of the individual reproductions?

Aggregation / analysis

  1. Were any reproductions excluded from the aggregation/analysis, and for what reasons?

  2. How did you evaluate reproducibility of the original studies? Describe the specific criteria and/or measure(s) used.

  3. What analysis methods and software did you use to evaluate reproducibility?

  4. Besides reproducibility measures (see item 39), did you analyze the results of your reproductions in other ways, and if so, how? Provide a full description of the (statistical) analysis.

  5. Did you conduct sensitivity analyses for the results of your reproducibility effort, and if so, which ones?

Data availability

  1. Are the materials (e.g. data and code) for your reproductions openly available? If so, where? If not, why not?

Discussion

  1. What are the conclusions of your reproducibility effort?

  2. Are these conclusions intended to be representative of a broader body of literature? If so, which one? Discuss constraints on generality due to the representativeness of the selected studies.

  3. What are the limitations of your reproducibility effort resulting from intrinsic features of the original studies? (e.g. temporal context, limited materials)

  4. What are the limitations of your reproducibility effort resulting from limitations of the reproductions? (e.g. availability of expertise, infrastructure, unplanned deviations from original studies and/or preregistration)

  5. Did you identify key factors that influenced the reproducibility outcome? Distinguish factors at the level of:

    1. the original studies, and

    2. the reproductions.

Authorship / contributions

  1. How were authorship contributions defined and attributed?

  2. How was the reproducibility effort funded?

  3. Did you obtain approval from an ethics committee? If so, for the whole reproducibility effort and/or for each reproduction individually?

  4. Are there any potential conflicts of interest?
